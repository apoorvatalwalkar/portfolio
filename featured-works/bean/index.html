PREVIOUS WORK

Context

For my final project for my Introduction to Computer Graphics, me and three of my classmates decided to try and raytrace the Chicago Bean. Raytracing is a rendering method that shoots rays out of the camera towards a scene and follows those rays as they bounce off shiny object and interact with lights. We had already built our own raytracers for earlier projects in the course and we were looking to build on this base.

WHAT WE IMPLEMENTED

Features

We decided to raytrace the Chicago Bean because it was a significant increase in complexity from our raytracer. Firstly, the shape of the bean is something we hadn't encountered previously. We implemented a .obj loader and imported complex meshes to be raytraced. We also had to create a complex scene made of buildings and people to show in the reflection. On top of this we implemented an acceleration structure, camera movement, occlusion, and motion blur.

WHAT I DID

My Contribution

Base Raytracer

We built the project around the raytracer I had coded for the previous projects in the class.

Acceleration Structure

Firstly, I implemented a BVH or Bounding Volume Hierarchy to speed up the raytracing process. What this does is create a cube around all of the objects in the scene to raytrace. We do this because a cube is much quicker to raytrace than sphere, cones, etc. because it's implicit equation just consists of 6 planes. Since we are raytracing such a complex scene, this reduced the rendering time significantly allowing us to run and test much faster.

Camera Movement

The second feature I implemented was the camera movement. This involved changing both the camera's position vector and the camera's look vector incrementally to rotate around the bean. This involved some trig to figure out how to move the camera around a circle without hardcoding the values or angle. And a lot of testing to change the increment at a small enough step to create the illusion of smooth movement.

FINAL

Results

Takeaways

I really enjoyed this project. I really deepened my understanding of raytracing and I learned how just a few small tweaks can make my raytracer much more realistic. I am extremely proud of the result my team was able to produce. We found that raytracing with an extremely complex bean took up to twenty minutes. So, to generate the 180 frames we wanted for a full circle (each being 2 degrees apart), we had to cut down on the complexity of the sphere.

In the future, I'd love to start the rendering earlier so we can produce all of the frames at that high complexity seen in the opening image or research other acceleration structures to cut down on rendering time even further.
